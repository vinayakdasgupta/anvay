Introduction

anvay is a web-based platform for topic modelling and interpretive analysis of Bengali-language texts. Note:Issues with processing Bengali

Processing Bengali for topic modelling involves a set of distinct constraints that users should keep in mind when interpreting results. Unlike English, Bengali exhibits rich inflectional morphology, frequent compound word forms, and variable spelling patterns ‚Äî especially in informal or older texts. The script also includes characters like zero-width joiners that complicate tokenisation, and there is no universally accepted stemming algorithm. These structural properties affect how words are grouped into topics.

Beyond the language itself, the type of corpus matters. A news corpus typically uses formulaic language, leading to clearer, more stable topic clusters ‚Äî but often with repeated framing phrases that may require custom stopwording. Literary texts, by contrast, are stylistically diverse and thematically layered. Characters shift voice, metaphors mask meaning, and topical boundaries blur. In such cases, even well-trained models may produce diffuse or overlapping topics.

anvay offers tools to handle some of these challenges ‚Äî including Bengali-specific preprocessing, configurable token filters, and detailed diagnostics. But no tool can fully resolve the ambiguity of language. Users are encouraged to view topic models not as definitive maps, but as exploratory guides: shaped by data, framed by parameters, and ultimately interpreted through human reading.

Topic Modelling

Topic modelling is a statistical method used to identify recurring themes in large text collections. Rather than categorising documents manually, topic models detect patterns of word co-occurrence and infer abstract themes based on them.

The most widely used algorithm is Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA), which makes two key assumptions:

Documents as Topic Mixtures

Can a single document contain multiple topics?

Yes ‚Äî LDA models each document as a blend of topics in different proportions. For example, one story might be 60% about migration and 40% about domestic life.

Topics as Word Distributions

How does the model define a topic without labels?

How Do Machines Imagine Topics?

anvay uses a topic modelling algorithm called Latent Dirichlet Allocation, or LDA. It's one of the most widely used methods for discovering thematic structure in large text corpora ‚Äî and it‚Äôs implemented here using the gensim Python library.

LDA is a probabilistic model that assumes every document is a mixture of latent ‚Äútopics,‚Äù and each topic is a distribution over words. It doesn‚Äôt ‚Äúunderstand‚Äù your texts ‚Äî it notices patterns. If certain words tend to appear together across documents, the model assumes they belong to the same thematic cluster.

Technically, every word in the corpus ‚Äî even common ones like ‚Äúthe‚Äù or ‚Äúbut‚Äù ‚Äî is assigned a topic distribution. But most pipelines (including ours) filter out high-frequency, low-meaning words before modelling. The topics you interpret are built from the more distinctive words that remain.

In other words, LDA doesn‚Äôt give you meaning. It gives you structure. It shows you where clusters of co-occurrence exist ‚Äî and leaves it to you, the reader, to decide which ones matter.

      1. Training Overview

      This section is designed to help you tune your topic model by reflecting on how the input data and training parameters shaped the model. It provides a snapshot of your corpus and the internal diagnostics logged during training. Used carefully, this is where interpretability begins.

      Corpus Statistics

      These values reflect the input that the model actually received, *after* all filtering and preprocessing. They help you assess whether your model is working with enough ‚Äî and the right kind of ‚Äî data.

      • Total Documents: Each file uploaded is treated as one document. Fewer than 30 documents usually leads to unstable, overgeneralised topics. Over 100 allows more granularity.
      • Average Document Length: If this is under 300 tokens, topics may feel thin or generic. In such cases, consider:
        • Reducing stopword or rare word removal
        • Disabling stemming
        • Merging short documents
      • Vocabulary Size: The number of unique tokens remaining after filtering. A range between 1,000‚Äì10,000 is often ideal for LDA.
        • Too low: try lowering no_below or disabling remove common words
        • Too high: consider adding custom stopwords or raising no_above
      Training Log

      These logs come directly from the LDA engine (gensim), grouped into stages. They‚Äôre not just for debugging ‚Äî they help you interpret model behaviour and stability.

      • Token Filtering: Shows how many terms were discarded by your frequency thresholds. If too many tokens are discarded, your topics may become incoherent.
      • Training Configuration: Confirms what values were used for parameters like alpha, eta, and chunksize. These affect sparsity and learning rate.
      • Perplexity Estimates: This is an internal measure of how surprised the model is by the data. Lower perplexity is usually better ‚Äî but not always. Watch for:
        • Steady decline and flattening: good sign ‚Äî model has stabilised
        • Sharp drops late in training: may suggest overfitting
        • Rising after flattening: potential overtraining ‚Äî try fewer passes
      • Final Model Info: Marks the lifecycle stages of the model. Mostly useful for confirming that training completed without interruption.
      What to Do With This
      • If your topics seem repetitive, generic, or incoherent, start by checking your average document length and vocabulary size.
      • If training logs show rapid convergence but poor topics, try increasing passes or adjusting alpha for more topic sparsity.
      • If perplexity never settles, it may indicate noisy input or a mismatch between the number of topics and the size of the corpus.
      • If you see ‚Äúkeeping N of M words‚Äù in token filtering, and N is very low, revisit your stopwords and frequency thresholds.
      Reminder: You don‚Äôt need to understand every log line. But if your output looks wrong, this is the first place to look ‚Äî and the best place to learn.

      anvay uses a probability threshold (minimum_probability = 0.1) when querying the model. This means:

      • Any topic that contributes less than 10% to a document will not appear in its weight vector.
      • This helps reduce noise and focus attention on more meaningful associations.

      Help & Errors

      This section answers common questions and explains issues that may arise when using anvay. Topic modelling is sensitive to your corpus and settings ‚Äî so if things seem off, you're not alone.

      Why does it say ‚ÄúDictionary is empty after filtering‚Äù?

      This means no words survived preprocessing. It usually happens when:

      • You set no_below too high or no_above too low
      • Your texts are too short, or your filters removed nearly everything
      • You combined heavy stopwording with rare word removal
      What to do: Lower no_below, reduce filtering, and check your documents are long enough.

      Why do all my topics look the same?

      If multiple topics use similar words or feel indistinguishable, it might be because:

      • Your number of topics is too high for the dataset
      • Common words haven‚Äôt been filtered out properly
      • Your documents follow a formula (like news intros or transcripts)
      What to do: Reduce the number of topics, filter common words more aggressively, or use a custom stopword list.

      Why were some of my documents skipped?

      Documents may be excluded if:

      • They are under ~100 tokens after preprocessing
      • They contain mostly filler words or invalid characters
      • They failed encoding validation (only UTF-8 is supported)
      What to do: Use longer texts, check your token counts, and clean noisy documents before uploading.

      Why isn‚Äôt anything happening when I click 'Anayse Topics?

      If the interface seems unresponsive, check whether:

      • You uploaded more than 800 files
      • Your total upload exceeds 10MB
      • You've accidentally included a non-.txt file or a file that isn‚Äôt UTF-8 encoded
      What to do: Remove problematic files, stay under the file and size limits, and ensure encoding is correct.

      Why are the results messy or unclear?

      This is a normal part of working with topic models. If topics feel noisy, repetitive, or unintelligible, it's often a sign that:

      • Your documents are too short or inconsistent
      • Your parameters (like alpha or eta) are poorly tuned
      • Your corpus needs better stopwording or filtering
      What to do: Iterate. Change one setting at a time, rerun, and compare. Interpretation improves with each pass.
