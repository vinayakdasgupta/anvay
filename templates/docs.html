Introduction

anvay is a web-based platform for topic modelling and interpretive analysis of text corpora. Built for scholars, students, and digital humanists, it provides an end-to-end workflow that enables users to upload a corpus, generate interpretable topic models, and explore their thematic structure through a suite of interactive visualisations and reports.

At its core, anvay uses Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) to uncover latent themes in textual corpora. It complements this algorithmic analysis with tools that foreground interpretability and readability — especially for morphologically rich and low-resource languages.

Note: Issues with Processing Text

Processing text for topic modelling involves constraints that vary significantly by language. No tool can fully resolve the ambiguity of language — users are encouraged to view topic models not as definitive maps, but as exploratory guides: shaped by data, framed by parameters, and ultimately interpreted through human reading.

Morphological Complexity

Bengali, Hindi, and Tamil are morphologically rich — inflected forms, compound constructions, and script-specific characters (like zero-width joiners in Bengali, or Tamil vowel markers) affect tokenisation quality. English is simpler in this regard, but OCR noise and domain jargon still matter.

Corpus Type Matters

News texts yield clearer clusters due to formulaic structure. Literary texts are more fluid, with shifting voices and metaphors that blur topical boundaries. This applies regardless of language — though the effect is often more pronounced in Bengali and Tamil literary corpora.

anvay's Approach

The platform applies language-specific preprocessing and topic diagnostics. Bengali has the most mature pipeline. English, Hindi, and Tamil are functional but experimental — results should be interpreted with additional caution and validated against the source texts.

Topic Modelling

Topic modelling is a statistical method used to identify recurring themes in large text collections. Rather than categorising documents manually, topic models detect patterns of word co-occurrence and infer abstract themes based on them.

The most widely used algorithm is Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA), which makes two key assumptions:

Documents as Topic Mixtures

Can a single document contain multiple topics?

Yes — LDA models each document as a blend of topics in different proportions. For example, one story might be 60% about migration and 40% about domestic life.

Topics as Word Distributions

How does the model define a topic without labels?

A topic is simply a group of words that tend to appear together. For example, a farming topic might include words for village, rice, land, cultivate, and farmer — in whatever script you are working in.

Interpretation Follows Modelling

Does the model know what the words mean?

No — the model detects statistical patterns, not meaning. It's the researcher who interprets the topics after modelling is complete.

Click to expand: Why Topic Modelling Requires Caution

Topic models operate on co-occurrence, not semantics. In noisy or morphologically complex corpora, models may group suffixes, filler, or OCR artefacts as if they were meaningful. Always combine quantitative exploration with qualitative judgement.

How Do Machines Imagine Topics?

anvay uses a topic modelling algorithm called Latent Dirichlet Allocation, or LDA. It's one of the most widely used methods for discovering thematic structure in large text corpora — and it's implemented here using the gensim Python library.

LDA is a probabilistic model that assumes every document is a mixture of latent "topics," and each topic is a distribution over words. It doesn't "understand" your texts — it notices patterns. If certain words tend to appear together across documents, the model assumes they belong to the same thematic cluster.

Technically, every word in the corpus — even common ones — is assigned a topic distribution. But most pipelines (including ours) filter out high-frequency, low-meaning words before modelling. The topics you interpret are built from the more distinctive words that remain.

In other words, LDA doesn't give you meaning. It gives you structure. It shows you where clusters of co-occurrence exist — and leaves it to you, the reader, to decide which ones matter.

      Reading Topics: Interpretation and Probability

      Topic modelling doesn't assign hard categories. Every word in every document is given a probability of belonging to each topic. Even a seemingly irrelevant word like "the" or "but" is mathematically linked to every topic — just with very low weight. Conversely, strongly associated words might belong to multiple topics with varying strengths.

      This means that "topics" are not neat buckets, but soft groupings — blurry constellations of co-occurrence. What feels like a coherent theme is, in fact, a pattern we detect and make sense of as readers. The model shows us structure, but not meaning. We impose that meaning by recognising which words rise to the top, and how they resonate with our sense of language, genre, or context.

      In literary texts, this becomes even more complex. Characters shift registers, metaphors disguise literal meaning, and themes bleed across documents. Topic models can surface patterns, but not always meanings. They require interpretation — and that interpretive labour is an essential part of working with LDA, especially in the humanities.

      Language-Specific Considerations
      BN Bengali
      Bengali includes zero-width joiners (U+200C, U+200D) that can break tokenisation. anvay strips these automatically, but copying from PDFs or older digital texts may introduce them unexpectedly. Hasanta (্), anusvara (ং), and vowel matras (া ি ী ু ূ) are preserved in filenames and display — make sure your source files are saved in UTF-8. Typical noise: পৃষ্ঠা ১, ***, speaker names like রামু বলল in dialogue.
      EN English Experimental
      English is the most straightforward to tokenise and clean. The main sources of noise are OCR artefacts, headers/footers from PDFs, and domain-specific jargon that inflates the vocabulary. Lemmatisation is available via WordNet. It reduces word forms (e.g. running → run, better → good) and is recommended for most English corpora. Typical noise: Page numbers, chapter headings, repeated boilerplate text.
      HI Hindi Experimental
      Hindi uses Devanagari script, which anvay handles with a custom tokeniser modelled on the Bengali approach. Vowel signs (मात्राएँ) and nukta characters are preserved. Important: NLTK does not provide a Hindi stopword list. You must upload a custom stopword file for meaningful results. Common particles like है, था, और, में should be included. Lemmatisation uses simplemma, which is dictionary-based and experimental for Hindi.
      TA Tamil Experimental
      Tamil has an agglutinative morphology — words are formed by chaining suffixes to roots, which makes stemming important. anvay uses a dictionary-based stemmer for Tamil. Important: Like Hindi, no NLTK Tamil stopword list is available. Upload a custom stopword file with common function words (e.g. அது, இது, மற்றும், என்று). Typical noise: Tamil pulli (virama) and aaytham (ஃ) characters are handled, but some OCR tools produce fragmented Tamil glyphs.

      Tip: Copying from PDFs may introduce invisible characters in any language. Always open your files in a text editor to verify they are saved in UTF-8 before uploading.

      Best practice: Use clean plain-text files, one file per document. Avoid headers, footers, and formatting markup of any kind.

      Uploading Files

      On the upload screen, drag and drop your .txt files or use the file picker. Each file you upload is treated as a separate document in the topic model. The more files you include, the more stable and interpretable your results — but there are practical limits to ensure performance and readability.

      • Maximum files: 800
      • Total upload size: 100 MB
      • File format: Plain text (.txt) only
      • Encoding: UTF-8 (mandatory for all languages)
      How File Uploads Work

      Each file represents one document. The model learns themes across the corpus by analysing word patterns across these documents. If your files are too short (under ~300 tokens), topics may feel weak or generic. If they are too long or unevenly distributed, dominant documents may skew topic prevalence.

      Use consistent file formatting and avoid uploading empty, duplicate, or placeholder files. File names are displayed in the Report tab, so name them meaningfully to track interpretations. Filenames in any language — Bengali, Hindi, Tamil — are fully supported and will display correctly in all charts.

      What Not to Upload
      • PDFs, Word docs, or ZIP files — only plain .txt files are accepted
      • Files with non-UTF-8 encodings — these may break preprocessing or display gibberish
      • System files (like .DS_Store) — these will be ignored or discarded
      Tips for Best Results
      • Use UTF-8 encoding (check in your text editor or use Save As → Encoding)
      • Avoid OCR scans or pasted content from PDFs unless cleaned (see Corpus Preparation)
      • If a document contains less than 100 tokens after filtering, it may be skipped automatically
      • Use short, descriptive filenames to help interpretation later — e.g. tagore_ghare_baire.txt, premchand_godan_ch1.txt
      Note: If your upload fails or stalls, check the file count and total size. Errors are often caused by large corpora, improper encoding, or unsupported file types.
      Stopwords

      Stopwords are high-frequency words — such as pronouns, particles, or auxiliary verbs — that tend to dilute topic modelling results. Removing them sharpens topic coherence by reducing statistical noise.

      How anvay Handles Stopwords

      anvay offers two mechanisms for stopword removal:

      1. Use the built-in NLTK stopword list
        Enable this by selecting Yes on the Remove Stopwords field. NLTK provides stopword lists for BN Bengali and EN English. For HI Hindi and TA Tamil, this option has no effect — NLTK does not include these languages.
      2. Upload your own stopword file
        In Advanced Options, upload a plain-text .txt file with one word per line. This is essential for Hindi and Tamil, and strongly recommended for all languages when working with domain-specific corpora.

      If both options are selected, the lists are automatically merged before modelling begins.

      Parameters

      The parameters you choose during upload affect how the model processes your texts and extracts topics. Some are intuitive, others more technical. Begin with recommended ranges — then adjust gradually based on the interpretability of your output.

      Basic Parameters

      Number of Topics
      This controls how many thematic clusters the model will attempt to identify. It's not a fixed setting — it depends heavily on your corpus:

      • Small, focused corpora (e.g., 30–50 short documents): try 5–10 topics
      • Medium corpora (100–200 documents with some variation): try 10–20
      • Large, diverse corpora (e.g., 500+ documents or full-length essays): you can try 30–50+, but interpret with care
      More topics isn't always better. Too many can produce overlapping, incoherent, or redundant results — especially if your documents are short or formulaic.

      Tip: If topics feel repetitive or unclear, reduce the number. If topics feel too broad, increase gradually. The goal is not coverage — it's clarity.

      Language
      Select the primary language of your uploaded documents. This controls which tokeniser, normaliser, and stopword list are used. Do not mix languages within a single run — upload multilingual corpora as separate analyses.

      BN Bengali: dictionary-based stemming, NLTK stopwords, zero-width character filtering EN English (exp.): WordNet lemmatisation, NLTK stopwords, alphabetic token filter HI Hindi (exp.): simplemma lemmatisation, custom stopwords only, Devanagari tokeniser TA Tamil (exp.): dictionary-based stemming, custom stopwords only, Tamil script tokeniser

      Normalisation
      Reduces word forms to a common base. The available options depend on language:

      • BN Stemming — reduces Bengali inflected forms via dictionary lookup (e.g. খাচ্ছে, খেয়েছে → খা). Recommended for most Bengali corpora.
      • EN Lemmatisation — reduces English words to dictionary forms (e.g. running → run). Recommended.
      • HI Lemmatisation — experimental Hindi lemmatisation via simplemma. Use cautiously.
      • TA Stemming — dictionary-based Tamil stemming. Recommended for Tamil corpora.
      Advanced Options

      Remove Top Words (%)
      Automatically discards the top N% most frequent words across the corpus. These often include generic verbs or connectors that don't add thematic value. Start with 5–10.

      Filter: No Below
      Removes words that appear in fewer than N documents. Useful to exclude rare noise — typos, one-off names, OCR artefacts. Recommended: 3–10.

      Filter: No Above
      Removes words that appear in more than this fraction of documents. These are often uninformative. Recommended: 0.5–0.7.

      Alpha and Eta
      These govern sparsity:

      • Alpha: Lower values → fewer topics per document (sharper focus)
      • Eta: Lower values → fewer words per topic (sharper boundaries)
      Example: In a 172-file Bengali news corpus, alpha = 0.5 and eta = 0.5 produced clean, interpretable topics. Outcomes depend on corpus structure — experiment to find what works best for your data.

      Chunk Size
      Controls how many documents are processed at once during training. This affects speed and memory use — not model quality. Default: 100–200.

      Multicore Training
      If enabled, anvay will use multiple CPU cores (if available) to speed up model training.

      Per-word Topics
      Stores which topic each individual word is most strongly associated with. This enables token-level visualisations. Slightly increases memory usage. Useful for interpretive close reading.
      Tip: If you're unsure where to begin, use the walkthrough settings — or anvay's defaults. The best way to improve your results is not to change everything at once. Tweak one parameter, rerun, then interpret.
      Visualisations

      Each visualisation in anvay helps you explore your topic model from a different angle: identifying overlap, strength, evolution, and prevalence.

      💡 Pro Tip: Hover over any chart to explore tooltips and interactive details. All charts use a single, globally consistent colour map — Topic 0 is always the first colour, Topic 1 the second, and so on. This makes cross-visual comparison reliable.

      Note: All visualisations display the top words for each topic on hover — making it easy to identify and interpret topics directly from the charts without switching back to the Report tab.

      1. Topic Similarity Scatterplot

      Closer dots = more similar topics. This chart plots topics based on their word distributions. Topics that are close together share many high-weight words. Use this to identify overlapping themes or cluster boundaries.

      Axes: Both axes represent a two-dimensional projection of topic–word distributions. Distance between points reflects topic similarity; the axes themselves have no intrinsic semantic meaning.

      2. Topic × Word Heatmap

      Brighter cells = stronger topic–word link. Each cell shows how strongly a word is associated with a topic. Use this to explore overlap and word distribution depth. This view presents the same information shown in the bar chart, but emphasises patterns and contrast across topics.

      Axes: The horizontal axis lists topic indices, and the vertical axis lists a globally selected set of high-weight words. Cell intensity reflects the strength of association between a word and a topic.

      3. Topic Prevalence Pie Chart

      Bigger slices = more dominant topics. This chart shows how much each topic contributes to the corpus overall.

      Encoding: Slice size represents the cumulative weight of a topic across the corpus.

      4. Top Word Frequency

      High bars = frequent words in your corpus. Use this to inspect lexical dominance and identify potential noise.

      Axes: The horizontal axis shows word tokens, and the vertical axis shows their frequency in the corpus after preprocessing.

      5. Topic–Word Network Graph

      Each topic node links to its top words. Blue circles are topics, grey ones are words. Helps visualise word overlap and outliers.

      Encoding: Spatial layout reflects relational structure rather than scale; proximity and connections indicate association, not distance or magnitude.

      6. Topic Evolution Over Time

      Each line shows a topic's weight across time. Use this to detect historical shifts or evolving discourse patterns.

      Axes: The horizontal axis represents document order (or a derived sequence such as time bins), and the vertical axis shows topic weight, tracing how topic presence changes across the corpus.

      Use case: For example, if a novel is divided into chapters and each chapter is uploaded as a separate file with filenames reflecting chapter order, this visualisation can be used to examine how thematic emphasis shifts across the course of the narrative.
      7. Topic Distribution Across Documents

      Each dot shows a topic's weight in one document. Useful for detecting niche vs. broad topics and skewed presence.

      Axes: The horizontal axis lists documents in the uploaded corpus, and the vertical axis shows topic weight, indicating how strongly each topic is represented in individual documents.

      8. Topic Distance (Clustering Dendrogram)

      Shorter branches = more similar topics. This dendrogram clusters topics by semantic similarity. Helps spot overlap or hierarchy.

      Axes: The vertical axis shows inter-topic distance, while the horizontal arrangement reflects hierarchical grouping rather than a quantitative scale.

      1. Training Overview

      This section is designed to help you tune your topic model by reflecting on how the input data and training parameters shaped the model. It provides a snapshot of your corpus and the internal diagnostics logged during training. Used carefully, this is where interpretability begins.

      Corpus Statistics

      These values reflect the input that the model actually received, after all filtering and preprocessing. They help you assess whether your model is working with enough — and the right kind of — data.

      • Total Documents: Each file uploaded is treated as one document. Fewer than 30 documents usually leads to unstable, overgeneralised topics. Over 100 allows more granularity.
      • Average Document Length: If this is under 300 tokens, topics may feel thin or generic. In such cases, consider reducing stopword or rare word removal, disabling stemming, or merging short documents.
      • Vocabulary Size: The number of unique tokens remaining after filtering. A range between 1,000–10,000 is often ideal for LDA.
      Training Log

      These logs come directly from the LDA engine (gensim), grouped into stages. They help you interpret model behaviour and stability.

      • Token Filtering: Shows how many terms were discarded by your frequency thresholds.
      • Training Configuration: Confirms what values were used for parameters like alpha, eta, and chunksize.
      • Perplexity Estimates: Lower perplexity is usually better. Steady decline and flattening = good; rising after flattening = potential overtraining.
      • Final Model Info: Marks the lifecycle stages of the model.
      What to Do With This
      • If your topics seem repetitive, generic, or incoherent, start by checking your average document length and vocabulary size.
      • If training logs show rapid convergence but poor topics, try increasing passes or adjusting alpha.
      • If perplexity never settles, it may indicate noisy input or a mismatch between the number of topics and corpus size.
      Reminder: You don't need to understand every log line. But if your output looks wrong, this is the first place to look — and the best place to learn.

      2. Top Words per Topic

      This section shows the top 10 words in each topic, ranked by their probability of occurring within that topic. These are taken directly from the model — no filtering, smoothing, or interpretation has been applied.

      In technical terms, this is based on p(w | t) — the probability of a word given a topic. The higher the value, the more representative that word is for that topic. However, this measure does not account for whether the word is distinctive across topics.

      How to Interpret
      • Repetition across topics: If the same word appears in many topics, it may be common across the corpus — not distinctive. Consider adding it to your custom stopwords.
      • Unusual terms: Some topics may include broken suffixes, particles, or OCR noise — signs of preprocessing issues, not model failure.
      • Use your judgement: These word lists are prompts. Try to label each topic yourself — what theme or discourse might these words suggest?
      • Check against documents: Return to the representative sentences. Do the top words actually appear in those contexts?
      Tip: These are the words the model "believes" belong to each topic — but the model doesn't understand meaning. That part is yours.

      3. Topic Weights per Document

      This section shows how each topic contributes to each document, based on the topic model's output. The weights are probabilistic, not categorical — every document is a mixture of topics, and every topic appears in multiple documents to varying degrees.

      Each value represents p(t | d) — the model's estimate of how likely topic t is to occur in document d. The sum of all topic weights for a single document is 1.0.

      How to Read This Table
      • High values (e.g. ≥ 0.6): indicate a document where that topic is dominant.
      • Flat distributions: If a document has many topics with small weights (e.g. 0.05–0.15), it may not fit cleanly into any one theme.
      • Repeated patterns: If the same topic dominates across many documents, your model may be too coarse.
      • Missing weights: A topic with 0.0 in a document does not appear there, or appears below the model's threshold.

      Example: Suppose a document has the following topic weights:

      Topic 2: 0.61, Topic 4: 0.24, Topic 7: 0.11

      Topic 2 is strongly dominant; Topic 4 plays a secondary role; Topic 7 is minor but present.

      Interpretation Tip: There is no single "main topic" per document. These weights are gradients, not labels. Think in terms of blends, not buckets.

      5. Topic Prevalence

      Topic prevalence indicates how strongly each topic appears across the entire corpus. It is calculated by summing the topic's weight across all documents. Although topic weights do not have a fixed scale, a higher value generally means that the topic appears more consistently and with greater intensity in the dataset.

      This table helps you identify which topics dominate the corpus overall — but note that very high values can sometimes reflect overfitting or topic redundancy. Use this view in combination with the document-level weights and top words to evaluate coverage and specificity.

      STEP 5: Learnings

      Bengali (and this applies broadly to all supported languages) is:

      • Morphologically rich
      • Rhetorically compressed
      • Culturally entity-centric

      Named entities, places, and idiosyncratic phrasing are not incidental — they are structural elements of meaning. Standard filtering often erases these, making latent themes disappear.

      What this walkthrough shows is that:
      Legibility is a function of filtering. A topic doesn't appear because it's important — it appears because your filtering method allows it to survive.

      What You Should Try

      anvay defaults to a gentler filtering method (removing only the top 10% of words) so you can surface latent structure. But you can also enable frequency filtering if your goal is topical readability.

      We recommend:

      • Start with no no_below/no_above, just remove top 5–10% words
      • Observe which topics emerge
      • Then apply filters to examine what vanishes and what persists

      This helps you understand your corpus — and your assumptions — much more than relying on a single run.

      Closing Thought

      Topic modelling is not neutral. The same corpus can yield very different "themes" depending on how you treat frequency. anvay is designed not to automate interpretation, but to reveal its conditions.

      Help & Errors

      This section answers common questions and explains issues that may arise when using anvay. Topic modelling is sensitive to your corpus and settings — so if things seem off, you're not alone.

      Why does it say "Dictionary is empty after filtering"?

      This means no words survived preprocessing. It usually happens when:

      • You set no_below too high or no_above too low
      • Your texts are too short, or your filters removed nearly everything
      • You combined heavy stopwording with rare word removal
      What to do: Lower no_below, reduce filtering, and check your documents are long enough.

      Why do all my topics look the same?

      If multiple topics use similar words or feel indistinguishable, it might be because:

      • Your number of topics is too high for the dataset
      • Common words haven't been filtered out properly
      • Your documents follow a formula (like news intros or transcripts)
      What to do: Reduce the number of topics, filter common words more aggressively, or use a custom stopword list.

      Why were some of my documents skipped?

      Documents may be excluded if:

      • They are under ~100 tokens after preprocessing
      • They contain mostly filler words or invalid characters
      • They failed encoding validation (only UTF-8 is supported)
      What to do: Use longer texts, check your token counts, and clean noisy documents before uploading.

      Why isn't anything happening when I click 'Analyse Topics'?

      If the interface seems unresponsive, check whether:

      • You uploaded more than 800 files
      • Your total upload exceeds 100 MB
      • You've accidentally included a non-.txt file or a file that isn't UTF-8 encoded
      What to do: Remove problematic files, stay under the file and size limits, and ensure encoding is correct.

      Why are the results messy or unclear?

      This is a normal part of working with topic models. If topics feel noisy, repetitive, or unintelligible, it's often a sign that:

      • Your documents are too short or inconsistent
      • Your parameters (like alpha or eta) are poorly tuned
      • Your corpus needs better stopwording or filtering
      What to do: Iterate. Change one setting at a time, rerun, and compare. Interpretation improves with each pass.

      I'm using Hindi or Tamil and getting very poor results. Why?

      Hindi and Tamil support is experimental. The most common cause of poor results is missing stopwords — neither language has a built-in NLTK stopword list, so you must upload a custom stopword file. Without it, the model will be dominated by function words and particles that carry no thematic value.

      Additionally, the normalisation pipelines (simplemma for Hindi, dictionary stemming for Tamil) are less mature than the Bengali pipeline. If results remain poor after adding stopwords, try disabling normalisation and running with a cleaner corpus.

      My filenames show only consonants — Bengali vowels are missing in the graphs.

      This was a bug in earlier versions of anvay where a filename sanitiser inadvertently stripped Unicode combining marks (matras and vowel signs). It has been fixed. If you are still seeing this, make sure you are running the latest version of app.py.

      Glossary

      Core Concepts
      Topic Modelling
      A method to uncover hidden themes in a collection of texts based on word co-occurrence patterns.
      LDA (Latent Dirichlet Allocation)
      A probabilistic model that treats each document as a mixture of topics, and each topic as a distribution over words.
      Topic
      A statistical cluster of words that frequently occur together. Interpreted as a "theme."
      Document
      A single text file uploaded to anvay. Each document is treated as a unit of analysis.
      Token
      An individual word or term, usually after preprocessing (normalisation, stemming, etc.).
      Language Support
      Bengali (BN)
      The primary supported language. Uses a custom zero-width-aware tokeniser, dictionary-based stemming, and NLTK stopwords.
      English (EN) — Experimental
      Supported with NLTK word tokenisation, WordNet lemmatisation, and NLTK English stopwords. Experimental status reflects limited testing on diverse corpora.
      Hindi (HI) — Experimental
      Uses a custom Devanagari tokeniser and simplemma lemmatisation. No built-in NLTK stopwords — a custom stopword file is required for meaningful results.
      Tamil (TA) — Experimental
      Uses a custom Tamil script tokeniser and dictionary-based stemming. No built-in NLTK stopwords — a custom stopword file is required.
      Experimental
      A label applied to English, Hindi, and Tamil support in anvay. Preprocessing pipelines are functional but have not been extensively validated. Use results with additional caution.
      Parameters & Preprocessing
      Stemming
      Reduces inflected words to their root forms. Available for Bengali (dictionary-based) and Tamil (dictionary-based). E.g. Bengali: খাচ্ছে → খা.
      Lemmatisation
      Reduces words to their dictionary base form. Available for English (WordNet) and Hindi (simplemma, experimental).
      Stopwords
      Frequently used words filtered out before modelling to reduce noise. NLTK provides lists for Bengali and English only. Hindi and Tamil require a custom file.
      Custom Stopwords
      A user-uploaded list of words to remove from the corpus. Must be a plain-text UTF-8 file, one word per line. Essential for Hindi and Tamil.
      Number of Topics
      The number of thematic clusters the model will generate. More topics = finer granularity.
      Remove Common Words
      An option to remove the top N% most frequent words in the corpus.
      Remove Rare Words
      An option to exclude words that appear in very few documents.
      no_below
      The minimum number of documents a word must appear in to be included in the model.
      no_above
      The maximum proportion of documents a word may appear in to be retained (e.g., 0.6 = 60%).
      Alpha
      Controls topic sparsity per document. Lower values = fewer dominant topics per document.
      Eta
      Controls word sparsity per topic. Lower values = sharper themes; higher = more diffuse.
      Chunk Size
      The number of documents processed in each training batch. Impacts speed and stability.
      Multicore Training
      Enables parallel processing across CPU cores. Recommended for large corpora.
      Per-word Topics
      Stores topic assignments at the word level. Enables token-level highlighting and diagnostics.
      UTF-8
      The required text encoding for all uploaded files. Ensures characters from all supported scripts — Bengali, Devanagari, Tamil — are preserved correctly.
      OCR Noise
      Unintended characters from scanning errors (e.g., ***, @#$%). Common in digitised texts in any language.
      Malformed Token
      Broken or partial words resulting from segmentation issues. Can occur in Bengali, Hindi, or Tamil when zero-width characters or virama markers are mishandled.
      Matra / Vowel Sign
      A combining character in Indic scripts (e.g. Bengali া ি ী, Hindi ा ि ी, Tamil ா ி ீ) that attaches to consonants to form syllables. These are Unicode category Mn (non-spacing marks) and must be preserved in filenames and processing.
      Model Output & Interpretation
      Topic Prevalence
      How much a topic contributes to the corpus overall. Measured by total topic weight.
      Top Terms
      The most representative words per topic, ranked by λ-score.
      Lambda Score
      A weighted metric combining term frequency and distinctiveness within a topic.
      Representative Paragraph
      A real paragraph from a document where the topic has high weight.
      Low-Confidence Topic
      A topic that appears noisy or incoherent. Not flagged — left to user interpretation.
      Low-Confidence Sentence
      A representative sentence that has little or no overlap with the top words of its topic. Flagged as ⚠ Low Confidence in the Report.
      Document–Topic Distribution
      A matrix showing how strongly each topic appears in each document.
      Topic Coherence
      A diagnostic metric (not computed by anvay) used to assess interpretability.
      Visualisation Types
      Scatterplot
      Plots topics in 2D space. Distance reflects topic similarity based on word distribution.
      Heatmap
      A grid showing word–topic weights. Brighter = stronger association.
      Pie Chart
      Displays topic prevalence visually. Larger slices = more dominant topics.
      Top Word Frequency
      Bar chart of the most frequent words across the corpus (after filtering).
      Topic–Word Network
      A graph showing links between topic nodes and their top terms.
      Topic Evolution
      A line chart showing topic prevalence over time or metadata bins.
      Topic Distribution
      Scatterplot showing how topics vary across documents.
      Dendrogram
      A tree-like diagram showing hierarchical similarity between topics.

      Visualisations

      Report
